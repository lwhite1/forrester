## Stocks are the memory of a system

Why do systems resist change? Why can't you fix a budget deficit overnight, stop an epidemic instantly, or drain a flooded reservoir in a moment?

The answer is **stocks**. A stock is a quantity that accumulates over time. It can only change through **flows** -- rates that add to or subtract from it. You can't teleport water out of a bathtub; it has to flow through the drain.

This simple constraint creates:

- **Inertia** -- stocks change gradually, not instantly
- **Delays** -- even after you turn off the faucet, the tub still holds water
- **Disequilibrium** -- stocks decouple inflows from outflows, so supply and demand don't have to match at every instant

Stocks are everywhere:

- **Population** -- changes through births and deaths
- **Bank balance** -- changes through deposits and withdrawals
- **Inventory** -- changes through production and shipments
- **Water in a reservoir** -- changes through rainfall and release

In this tutorial, you'll work with two models -- a bathtub and an exponential growth population -- to build deep intuition for how stocks and flows behave.
