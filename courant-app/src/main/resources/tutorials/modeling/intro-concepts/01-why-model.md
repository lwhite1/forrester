## Why do systems surprise us?

The world is full of systems that behave in ways we don't expect. Populations grow faster than anyone planned for. Epidemics explode and then fade. Supply chains oscillate wildly even when customer demand is steady. Stock markets crash despite no single triggering event.

Our intuition works well for simple, linear cause-and-effect. But most real-world problems involve **feedback**, **delays**, and **accumulation** — and human intuition handles these poorly.

**System Dynamics** (SD) is a method for understanding complex systems by building simulation models. It was founded by **Jay Forrester** at MIT in the 1950s, originally to study industrial supply chains. The key insight: you can represent a system's structure in a computer model, simulate its behavior over time, and test policies before applying them in the real world.

## What modeling gives you

- **Clarity** — forces you to state assumptions explicitly
- **Safety** — test interventions in simulation before reality
- **Surprise** — reveals counter-intuitive behavior hiding in feedback loops
- **Communication** — a shared, testable representation that teams can debate

By the end of this tutorial, you'll understand the core ideas that make System Dynamics different from other approaches: mental models, feedback thinking, stocks and flows, and the endogenous point of view.

*Why do some systems behave in ways we don't expect?* That is the question System Dynamics was invented to answer.
