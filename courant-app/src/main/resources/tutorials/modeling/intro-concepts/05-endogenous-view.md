## Explain behavior from within

System Dynamics takes an **endogenous** point of view — it seeks to explain a system's behavior as arising from its own internal structure, not from external shocks.

This doesn't mean the outside world doesn't matter. External events certainly happen: a pandemic arrives, a competitor enters the market, a regulation changes. But the system's **response** to those events — whether it recovers, collapses, or oscillates — is determined by its internal feedback structure.

## An example

Consider an inventory system. A sudden spike in customer orders is an external event. But the resulting oscillation in inventory and production — the **bullwhip effect** — is not caused by customers. It is caused by the delays and feedback loops inside the supply chain itself. A different supply chain structure, facing the same demand spike, would respond differently.

## Why this matters

The endogenous view has a practical consequence: **leverage lies inside the system**. If behavior is generated internally, then changing the system's structure — its feedback loops, delays, and decision rules — can change its behavior permanently. Blaming external events leads to reactive, short-term fixes. Understanding internal structure leads to lasting improvement.

This is why SD modelers ask: *What is it about this system's structure that produces the behavior we observe?* The answer is always found in the stocks, flows, and feedback loops that make up the system.
