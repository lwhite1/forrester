## Behavior comes from structure

The central idea of System Dynamics is that a system's **behavior over time** is generated by its **feedback structure**. Feedback means that the output of a process loops back to influence its own input.

There are two types of feedback loops:

## Reinforcing loops (R)

A **reinforcing loop** amplifies change. The more you have, the more you get — a snowball effect.

**Example — Bank account:** Interest is added to your balance. A larger balance earns more interest, which increases the balance further. Left alone, this produces **exponential growth**. The loop reinforces itself.

> Balance &rarr; Interest &rarr; Balance &rarr; ...

## Balancing loops (B)

A **balancing loop** resists change and drives the system toward a goal. It is self-correcting.

**Example — Thermostat:** When the room temperature drops below the setpoint, the heater turns on. As the room warms, the gap shrinks and the heater slows down. The loop pushes temperature toward the setpoint — **goal-seeking behavior**.

> Temperature Gap &rarr; Heating &rarr; Room Temperature &rarr; Temperature Gap &rarr; ...

## Why this matters

Most real systems contain both reinforcing and balancing loops. The **dominant loop** at any moment determines the behavior you observe — growth, decline, oscillation, or equilibrium. Understanding which loop is dominant, and when dominance shifts, is the core skill of System Dynamics.
