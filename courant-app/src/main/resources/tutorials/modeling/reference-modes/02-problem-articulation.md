## Define the problem before building the model

**Problem articulation** defines the scope. Don't build a model of "everything" -- focus on a specific dynamic problem.

## Questions to answer

- **What problem** are you trying to understand? Be specific.
- **What is the time horizon?** Weeks? Years? Decades?
- **Who are the stakeholders?** Whose behavior matters?
- **What variables are key?** Which outputs will you examine?
- **What behavior is problematic?** Growth that should stabilize? Oscillation that should be damped?

## Write a problem statement

Formulate a clear question: *"Why does X behavior occur, and what policies could change it?"*

Examples:

- "Why does our inventory oscillate, and how can we stabilize it?"
- "Why does urban population overshoot carrying capacity, and what infrastructure policies could prevent collapse?"
- "Why do product adoption curves follow an S-shape, and what accelerates the transition?"

## Why this matters

A clear problem statement constrains the model boundary. Without it, modelers include everything they know about the system, producing large models that are hard to test and harder to learn from. The problem statement tells you what to include -- and what to leave out.
